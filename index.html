About

I am a Ph.D. student in Economics at the Paris School of Economics (PSE) and Gender Coordinator at the World Inequality Lab (WIL). During the fall semester of 2025, I visited the London School of Economics (LSE) at the International Inequality Institute (III).

My research interests include inequality, intergenerational mobility, development economics, and political economy. My supervisors are François Bourguignon and Carlos Winograd.

Prior to joining PSE, I worked at the Inter-American Development Bank (IDB), the Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD), and public institutions in Argentina. I hold a Master in Applied Economics from Paris School of Economics and a BSc in Economics from Torcuato Di Tella University.

Working Paper

Working paper

Climbing the Ladder? How Latin Americans See Their Economic Mobility

Presentations: Applied Economics Lunch Seminar, Paris School of Economics, 2024 — Doctorissimes, Paris School of Economics, 2025 — Internal GPET Seminar, Paris School of Economics, 2025 — Stone Center – IPEA – LSE Summer School, Brasília, 2025 — London Inequality Group, King's College London, 2025 — WIL Conference, Paris School of Economics, 2026 — Second International Conference on Inequalities and Opportunities, Bari, 2026.

Read abstract

This paper documents perceived individual intergenerational mobility in 18 Latin American countries. It uses highly comparable and previously unexploited survey questions, where respondents report their own and their parents' economic status, providing a direct measure of how individuals experience their own intergenerational trajectory. Results show that perceived individual mobility aligns, in order of magnitude, with existing intergenerational income elasticities in the region, as perceived and income-based measures lie within the same range. However, the scope and the length of the dataset uncover a number of new facts. Although perceived individual mobility is low in Latin America, it is highly heterogeneous across countries, with Central American countries displaying the lowest levels and countries in the Southern Cone, the highest. Perceived mobility fluctuates over time between 2000 and 2020, but shows no clear trend except in Chile and Peru, where it declines sharply. For the latter, a complementary cohort analysis corroborates this trend, as younger cohorts tend to report lower mobility than older ones.
